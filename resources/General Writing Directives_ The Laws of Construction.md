## **I. General Writing Directives: The Laws of Construction**

**Directive 1: The Law of R.U.E. (Resist the Urge to Explain)**

* **Law:** Never explain a character’s emotion or the "meaning" of a scene if the action has already demonstrated it. The author’s secondary goal is to stir emotion, not describe it.  
* **Mathematical Constraint:** $1 \+ 1 \= 1/2$. If you show an action and then explain it, the explanation halves the impact of the action.  
* **Example:**  
  * *Fail:* Sam was mad. He pounded the table. "Rita, you're driving me crazy," she said angrily.  
  * *Pass:* Sam pounded the table. "Rita, you're driving me crazy."

**Directive 2: The Cradle-to-Grave Mandate**

* **Law:** The system must generate a complete history for every primary character from "cradle to grave" before drafting. The story on the page is only a strategic selection of these events.  
* **Requirement:** Identify the character's **Ghost**—the past traumatic event that creates their current internal struggle.

**Directive 3: The Law of Causality**

* **Law:** A story is not a series of "and then" events; it is a chain of "therefore" or "but" events linked by the law of cause and effect.  
* **Constraint:** Every scene must contain: $Event \+ Change \+ Conflict$. If a scene does not change the character’s situation, it is dead weight.

## **II. Creative & Story-Driven Directives: The 5-Act and 22-Step Architectures**

**Directive 4: The Fractal Structure Protocol (Yorke)**

* **Law:** Structure is fractal. The pattern of the whole story ($Exposition \\rightarrow Midpoint \\rightarrow Climax \\rightarrow Resolution$) must repeat within every act and every individual scene.  
* **The 5-Act Narrative Roadmap:**  
  1. **Act 1 (No Knowledge):** Introduce the protagonist in their "Ordinary World." They have a problem/flaw but lack the knowledge to fix it.  
  2. **Act 2 (Doubt/Overcoming Reluctance):** The protagonist is compelled into the "Woods" (the unfamiliar story world). They initially resist the necessary change.  
  3. **Act 3 (Experimenting/Midpoint):** The protagonist finds "Key Knowledge" at the midpoint. This is the hinge; everything after is consequences.  
  4. **Act 4 (Regression):** The protagonist has changed, but not enough. They face a "low moment" where their weakness is revealed and they fail to achieve their goal.  
  5. **Act 5 (Total Mastery):** The protagonist overcomes their Act 1 problem by resolving their internal flaw.

**Directive 5: The Character-Moral Engine (Truby)**

* **Law:** The story "walks" on two legs: **Acting** (pursuing a surface desire) and **Learning** (gaining moral self-revelation).  
* **The 7 Core Structural Steps:**  
  1. **Weakness/Need:** A moral or psychological flaw ruining the character's life.  
  2. **Desire:** The specific goal the hero wants to achieve (the spine of the story).  
  3. **Opponent:** The person best able to attack the hero's specific weakness.  
  4. **Plan:** The hero's blueprint to achieve the desire (which must fail at first).  
  5. **Battle:** The final conflict between the hero and the opponent.  
  6. **Self-Revelation:** A moment during the battle where the hero realizes the truth of their flaw.  
  7. **New Equilibrium:** The character is shown in a transformed state.

**Directive 6: Four-Corner Opposition**

* **Law:** Do not rely on a single villain. Create a "Character Web" where three or more opponents attack the hero's weakness from different value-based angles.  
* **Constraint:** Each opponent must conflict not only with the hero but also with every other character in the web.

**Directive 7: The Inciting Incident Timing Rule (Bernhardt)**

* **Law:** The Inciting Incident must radically upset the protagonist’s life and instigate the journey toward the goal.  
* **Constraint:** This event must occur within the first **20 pages** of the narrative.

## **III. Sales & Copywriting Directives: Persuasion through Structure**

*Note: As these source texts focus on fiction, these rules apply Truby and Yorke's structural logic to the "Story of the Sale."*

**Directive 8: The Sales-as-Journey Archetype**

* **Law:** Treat the customer as the Protagonist and the product as the "Key Knowledge" found at the Midpoint.  
* **Strategy:**  
  * **Phase 1 (Act 1):** Define the customer’s **Weakness/Need** (the problem ruining their current "Ordinary World").  
  * **Phase 2 (Act 3/Midpoint):** Introduce the product as the "Solution in the Woods."  
  * **Phase 3 (Act 5):** Present the **New Equilibrium** (life after the product).

**Directive 9: The Moral Argument for Conversion**

* **Law:** A pitch is a **Moral Argument**. You are making a claim through the "actions" (benefits) of the product.  
* **Technique:** Contrast the **Façade** (what competitors promise) against the **True Need** (the deep problem your product solves).

**Directive 10: Specificity over Platitude**

* **Law:** Generalities roll off the reader; quantifiable statements carry inherent proof.  
* **Example:**  
  * *Fail:* This service is very fast.  
  * *Pass:* This service cuts reporting from 3 hours to 20 minutes.

**Directive 11: The "Takeaway" Mandate**

* **Law:** Every output must answer the "Red Sneaker" question: "What is the takeaway?"  
* **Requirement:** Explicitly define how the reader is rewarded for spending time on this text. When they finish, they must feel a specific emotion and think a specific thought.

**Directive 12: The Hook and the Slide**

* **Law:** Use the **Inciting Event** (a spark that brings the situation from bad to worse) to challenge the reader into immediate action.  
* **Strategy:** Start in the middle of a conflict (In Media Res) and use "Beats" (action/reaction pairs) to propel the reader to the next sentence.
