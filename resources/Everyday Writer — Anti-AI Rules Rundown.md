# **Everyday Writer — Anti-AI Rules Rundown**

The core premise: AI writing has fingerprints. Specific words, sentence patterns, and structural habits that appear so consistently they've become tells. This document names them, shows what they look like in the wild, and shows what they look like fixed.

---

## **WORD CHOICE**

### **Magic Adverbs**

Adverbs injected to make weak observations feel important. If you need the adverb, the claim is the problem.

❌ "This fundamentally changes how we think about work." ✅ "This changes how we think about work." — or better, prove *how* it changes things.

### **Delve & Friends**

Words that went from rare to everywhere the moment LLMs went mainstream: *delve, utilize, leverage, robust, streamline, harness, certainly.*

❌ "We can leverage this framework to streamline our process." ✅ "We can use this to cut three steps out of the process."

### **Grandiose Nouns**

Abstract nouns that gesture at analysis without doing any: *tapestry, landscape, paradigm, synergy, ecosystem, framework.*

❌ "The landscape of modern finance is shifting." ✅ "Hedge funds are losing ground to retail platforms."

### **"Serves As" Dodge**

Using *serves as, stands as, marks, represents* instead of just saying *is.*

❌ "This moment serves as a turning point." ✅ "This is a turning point."

## **SENTENCE STRUCTURE**

### **Negative Parallelism**

"It's not X — it's Y." The single most common AI tell. One per piece is acceptable. Two is a pattern. Three is a confession.

❌ "It's not about working harder — it's about working smarter. It's not about doing more — it's about doing less, better." ✅ "The leverage is in what you cut, not what you add."

### **Dramatic Countdown**

"Not X. Not Y. Just Z." Nobody speaks this way unless they're doing a TED talk from a template.

❌ "Not luck. Not connections. Just relentless execution." ✅ "Relentless execution is what separated them."

### **Rhetorical Self-Q\&A**

The model asks a question nobody asked, then answers it.

❌ "The result? A company worth $10 billion. The lesson? Never ignore the fundamentals." ✅ "They built a $10 billion company by doing the boring thing everyone else skipped."

### **Filler Transitions**

*It's worth noting, Importantly, Interestingly, Notably, It bears mentioning.* Throat-clearing in text form. Delete them — the sentence either stands without them or it didn't need to exist.

❌ "Importantly, this affects every writer regardless of experience level." ✅ "This affects every writer regardless of experience level."

### **Tacked-On Analysis**

"-ing" phrases bolted to the end of sentences to simulate depth.

❌ "He left the company in 2019, marking a significant shift in leadership." ✅ "He left the company in 2019\. The board replaced him within a month."

### **False Ranges**

"From X to Y" where X and Y don't form a real spectrum.

❌ "From innovation to cultural transformation, AI is changing everything." ✅ "AI is changing hiring, writing, and drug discovery — and doing it at the same time."

## **PARAGRAPH STRUCTURE**

### **Short Punchy Fragments for Manufactured Emphasis**

One-sentence paragraphs used so often they lose all weight.

❌ "The market shifted. Nobody saw it coming. Everything changed overnight." ✅ Weave it into a paragraph. The rhythm earns its emphasis when it's not constant.

### **Listicle in a Trench Coat**

Prose that's secretly a bullet list: "The first thing... The second thing... The third thing..."

❌ "The first reason is cost. The second is time. The third is talent." ✅ "It costs too much, takes too long, and requires skills most teams don't have."

## **TONE**

### **False Suspense**

"Here's the kicker / Here's the thing / Here's what most people miss." Promises a revelation, delivers a normal observation.

❌ "Here's the thing — most writers don't actually read." ✅ "Most writers don't actually read."

### **Futurism Invitation**

"Imagine a world where..." — the AI pitch deck opener. Nobody imagines the world you're about to describe. State the thesis.

❌ "Imagine a world where every writer had a personal editor available 24/7." ✅ "Claude is a 24/7 editor. Most writers still don't use it properly."

### **False Vulnerability**

Simulated self-awareness that's polished, risk-free, and scheduled. Real vulnerability is specific and uncomfortable.

❌ "And yes, I'll admit — I've struggled with this too." ✅ "I spent six months writing essays nobody read because I was optimizing for length, not argument."

### **Stakes Inflation**

Everything is world-historical. "Will fundamentally reshape", "defines the next era", "entirely new paradigm."

❌ "This will fundamentally reshape the future of human creativity." ✅ "This makes certain creative jobs cheaper to do badly and harder to do well."

### **Pedagogical Voice**

"Let's break this down / Let's unpack / Let's dive in." Assumes the reader needs hand-holding.

❌ "Let's unpack why this matters." ✅ Start the argument. The reader will follow.

### **Vague Attributions**

"Experts argue", "studies suggest", "observers have noted." Name the expert. Link the study. If you can't, you don't have a source — you have a gesture at authority.

❌ "Experts agree that consistency is the key to building an audience." ✅ "Kevin Kelly's 1,000 True Fans essay makes this argument better than most:"

## **FORMATTING**

### **Em-Dash Addiction**

2–3 per piece is fine. 10+ is a tell. Restructure the sentence or use a comma.

### **Bold-First Bullets**

Every bullet starting with a bolded phrase is a documentation template tic, not a writing choice. If bullets need hierarchy, restructure the content.

---

## **COMPOSITION**

### **Fractal Summaries**

Summarizing every section, then the whole piece. "In this section we'll cover..." then "As we've seen..." Write forward. Don't recap.

### **One-Point Dilution**

The same argument restated 10 times across 4,000 words dressed up as comprehensiveness.

If the thesis is sound, say it once. Then add evidence, counterargument, and implication. Don't paraphrase your own thesis and call it depth.

### **Signposted Conclusion**

"In conclusion / To sum up / In summary." Competent writing doesn't announce its ending. The reader can feel it arriving. Delete the signpost; write the conclusion.

### **"Despite Its Challenges"**

Acknowledging problems only to immediately dismiss them.

❌ "Despite its challenges, AI represents an enormous opportunity. Despite these concerns, the future looks bright." ✅ Either engage the challenges or remove the pretense of balance.

---

## **QUICK CHECKLIST**

Before posting anything, run this:

* \[ \] Any magic adverbs? (quietly, deeply, fundamentally, remarkably)  
* \[ \] Any delve / utilize / leverage / robust / harness?  
* \[ \] More than one "It's not X — it's Y"?  
* \[ \] Any "Not X. Not Y. Just Z." countdown?  
* \[ \] Any rhetorical self-Q\&A?  
* \[ \] Three or more anaphora sentences in a row?  
* \[ \] Any "It's worth noting / Importantly / Notably"?  
* \[ \] Any tacked-on "-ing" analysis phrases?  
* \[ \] Any "Here's the kicker / thing / deal"?  
* \[ \] "Imagine a world where..." opener?  
* \[ \] More than 3 em-dashes?  
* \[ \] Bold-first bullet points throughout?  
* \[ \] Conclusion starting with "In conclusion" or "To sum up"?  
* \[ \] Does the piece end having *proved* something, or just *restated* it?
