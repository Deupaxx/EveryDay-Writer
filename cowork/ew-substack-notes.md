# EVERYDAY WRITER — SUBSTACK NOTES SKILL

**Part of the Everyday Writer system. Upload this file to a Claude.ai Project alongside `ew-master.md`, or use standalone.**

**To activate:** Tell Claude "use the Substack Notes skill" or "write me a Substack Note using Everyday Writer."

---

## WHAT THIS SKILL IS FOR

Substack Notes is a discovery layer, not a second newsletter. It lives between X and a full post — longer than a tweet, shorter than an issue, more conversational than either.

Notes that work are fragments with actual ideas inside them. A thought that didn't make it into the newsletter but is too good to waste. A reaction to something you read. A question you're genuinely sitting with.

Notes that don't work are newsletters cut into smaller pieces, or promotional announcements dressed as thoughts.

---

## BEFORE WRITING: APPLY THE ANTI-AI RULES

These rules are mandatory. Run the checklist before presenting any output.

### Banned Words — Delete on Sight

delve, utilize, leverage, robust, streamline, harness, certainly, notably, importantly, fundamentally, remarkably, deeply, foster, empower, showcase, underscore, highlight, tapestry, landscape, paradigm, synergy, cornerstone, stands as, serves as, marks a pivotal moment, represents a significant shift, It's worth noting, Importantly, Notably

### Patterns to Kill in Notes

**Context before the idea:**
❌ "I was reading something earlier this week that got me thinking about how we approach..."
✅ "The most-retweeted writing advice I've seen this year is also the most useless."

**Promotional disguise:** A Note that exists to announce something is not a Note. It's a press release dressed as a thought.

**CTA disguise:** "Subscribe to my newsletter to read the full piece" dressed as a Note. The Note must stand alone.

**Newsletter in disguise:** If it takes more than 3 paragraphs to make sense, it's a newsletter issue. Let it grow into one.

### Pre-Post Checklist

- [ ] Any banned words?
- [ ] Does it open with the idea, not the context?
- [ ] Is it a genuine fragment of thought or a newsletter issue squeezed small?
- [ ] Is it short enough? (100–400 words)
- [ ] If it links to something: is there actual perspective, not just a pointer?
- [ ] No "subscribe to my newsletter" CTA dressed as a thought?

## THE DISTINCTION FROM FULL POSTS

**A newsletter issue:** formal argument, built out, structured for sustained attention.

**A Note:** fragment, opinion, question, or observation — valuable precisely because it's incomplete enough to invite a response. The incompleteness is not a failure; it's the feature.

Think of Notes as the margin notes of your writing life. The thoughts you'd share with someone smart over coffee that don't need a 1,500-word essay to justify existing.

---

## WHAT NOTES ARE FOR

**Thinking out loud on something you haven't resolved yet.**
Not a conclusion — a genuine question you're working through. The reader should feel like they caught you mid-thought, not like they're reading a prepared statement.

**Link commentary with actual perspective.**
Not "Great piece by [Name]" — that's noise. "This essay by [Name] on [topic] is right about X but completely misses Y, which is the part that actually matters for [specific reason]." The link is the citation; your perspective is the Note.

**An observation that doesn't need development.**
Something you noticed that's complete in one or two paragraphs. Sharp, specific, standalone. If it needs more than 3 paragraphs, it's a newsletter issue in disguise.

**A question that invites actual conversation.**
Specific enough that readers have a concrete answer to give. "What do you think about AI?" is not a conversation-starter. "What's the one use of AI that's actually improved your writing process, not just your output?" is.

---

## FORMAT AND LENGTH

**Open with the idea, not the context.**
❌ "I was reading something earlier this week that got me thinking about how we approach..."
✅ "The most-retweeted writing advice I've seen this year is also the most useless."

**Short paragraphs, real line breaks.**
Notes are read on mobile. White space is not a formatting choice — it's a readability requirement.

**Length:** 100–400 words. Shorter is better if the idea is complete. Longer is fine if each paragraph earns it. Never pad a Note to look substantive — it reads as insecurity.

**No bold-first bullets.** If you need bullets in a Note, collapse them into a sentence.

---

## THE DISCOVERY FUNCTION

Notes serve your newsletter's growth because they reach readers who haven't found your main publication yet. Every Note is a sample of your thinking. It should represent the quality of the newsletter, not the scraps left over from it.

**The best Notes create a natural pull toward the newsletter:** not by saying "subscribe to my newsletter" — but by demonstrating a quality of thinking that makes the reader want more. The advertisement is the work itself.

**What to link, when:** Link to a full issue when the Note expands on something the issue covers — not as self-promotion, as a genuine pointer to more depth. If the link feels forced, skip it.

---

## VOICE ON NOTES

Notes are where your voice can be most unguarded. Use that. The newsletters get polished; the Notes get to be immediate.

Unguarded doesn't mean unthought. The best Notes feel spontaneous but aren't careless. The thought behind them is real; the presentation is just relaxed.
