---

## WHAT THIS SKILL IS FOR

Scene-level fiction writing. The mechanics of building scenes that move, breathe, and pull the reader forward. This is not about plot — plot is what happens. This is about how to make what happens feel real, urgent, and inevitable.

A scene is the basic unit of fiction. A novel is not a collection of chapters — it is a chain of scenes, each one connected to the next by consequence. Get the scene right and the rest follows.

---

## BEFORE WRITING: APPLY THE ANTI-AI RULES

These rules are mandatory for fiction. AI patterns break reader immersion — a single "marking a significant shift" in a fiction scene destroys the bubble. Run the checklist before presenting any draft.

### Banned Words and Patterns — Delete on Sight

delve, utilize, leverage, notably, importantly, fundamentally, remarkably, deeply, tapestry, landscape, paradigm, cornerstone, stands as, serves as, marks a pivotal moment, represents a significant shift, It's worth noting, Importantly, Notably

**Tacked-on -ing Analysis (especially dangerous in fiction):**
"...marking a significant turning point in her understanding."
"...revealing the depth of his grief."
"...underscoring the tension between them."
Cut these. Every time. If the scene does its job, the reader draws the inference.

**Announcing Emotions Instead of Showing Them:**
❌ "She was devastated." "He felt afraid." "The room seemed oppressive."
✅ Write the physical, observable detail that produces the emotion.

**R.U.E. violations (Resist the Urge to Explain):**
If you showed it, do not then state it. "She was furious — he could see that now." — cut "he could see that now." Trust what you built.

### Pre-Draft Checklist

- [ ] Any banned words?
- [ ] Any tacked-on -ing analysis phrases?
- [ ] Any emotions announced instead of shown?
- [ ] Any R.U.E. violations — showing then stating?
- [ ] Any passive voice where an active subject exists?
- [ ] Does the scene open at the last possible moment?
- [ ] Does the scene exit immediately when its work is done?

## THE SCENE CONTRACT

Every scene must earn its existence. The test is brutal and simple: if you removed this scene, would the story change? Not just lose color — actually change? If the answer is no, the scene is dead weight. Cut it or rebuild it.

**Every scene must contain three things (Yorke's Law):**
1. **Event** — something happens
2. **Change** — the situation is different at the end than at the start
3. **Conflict** — a force opposing the character's goal, external or internal

A scene that opens and closes at the same emotional or situational level is not a scene — it is description dressed as action.

---

## SCENE ENTRY AND EXIT

**Enter late. Leave early.**

Enter at the last possible moment before the conflict begins. The reader does not need setup.

❌ "Sarah arrived at the coffee shop at 2pm. She found a table near the window and ordered a latte. When Marcus walked in, she noticed he looked tired."
✅ "Marcus sat down without taking off his coat. Sarah knew then that this was going to be short."

Exit the scene the moment its work is done. Cut immediately after the beat that pays off the conflict. Do not explain what it means. The reader's imagination does the work between scenes. Trust it.

## THE BEAT STRUCTURE

A scene is built from beats. A beat is an action/reaction pair — one character acts, another responds, and the situation advances or complicates.

**How beats work:**
- Character A wants something
- Character A acts toward that want (action)
- Character B (or circumstance) responds (reaction)
- The situation is now different
- Repeat until the scene's conflict resolves or escalates

Beats are not always visible. An internal realization is a beat. A silence is a beat. A character deciding not to say the thing they almost said is a beat. What matters is that each one changes something.

**Beat pace:** Fast scenes have short beats and quick turns. Slow scenes have longer beats where each action is weighted. Match the rhythm to the emotional temperature.

## SHOW DON'T TELL: THE ACTUAL MECHANICS

"Telling" announces the emotional conclusion: "She was furious." "He was afraid."

"Showing" gives the reader the sensory data that produces the conclusion in their own mind. The inference the reader draws is theirs — which means it lands harder than the one you hand them.

**The formula:** Write the physical, observable, specific detail that carries the emotion — not the emotion itself.

❌ "He was nervous before the meeting."
✅ "He checked his phone twice in the elevator. When the doors opened he was still looking at the screen."

❌ "The house felt abandoned."
✅ "A pizza box sat on the radiator, its grease soaking through the cardboard. Nobody had moved it."

**R.U.E. — Resist the Urge to Explain.** If you've shown it, do not then state it. Cut the explanation. Trust what you built.

## SENSORY GROUNDING

Scenes that feel real are anchored in specific sensory detail. Not generic — specific.

"The room smelled like cigarettes" is generic. "The room smelled like the inside of a vending machine — burnt plastic and artificial sugar" is specific.

**The one telling detail.** Find the one detail that carries the scene's emotional truth and render it precisely. A half-eaten pizza on a radiator tells us more about the state of a relationship than three paragraphs of backstory.

**Sensory hierarchy in tense scenes:** Sound and touch are more visceral than sight. Smell is the most memory-triggering sense. Vision is the most overused. Vary the sensory entry point by scene.

---

## DIALOGUE

Dialogue in fiction is not transcribed conversation. It is compressed, purposeful, and loaded with subtext.

**The subtext rule:** Characters almost never say exactly what they mean in high-stakes scenes. The gap between what is said and what is meant is where the tension lives.

❌ "I don't trust you anymore," she said.
✅ "I saw you at the restaurant." A pause. "Which restaurant?" "You know which one."

**Silence as dialogue:** The beat where nothing is said is often the loudest beat.

**The "said" rule:** "Said" is invisible. Don't replace it with "remarked," "stated," "declared," "questioned." Use "said." When action makes attribution clear, skip the tag entirely.

**Dialogue and action:** Characters do things while they talk. These actions either reinforce or contradict what's being said. Contradiction is more interesting.

---

## POINT OF VIEW (POV)

POV is whose experience filters every sentence. Get inside the POV character's specific sensory and emotional reality and stay there.

**POV violations:**
- Describing what the POV character cannot see or know
- Giving the POV character a reaction they haven't earned yet
- Head-hopping within a scene

**The close POV advantage:** The tighter the POV, the higher the tension. In close third or first person, the reader only knows what the character knows. Use it.

---

## SCENE PACING

**Dramatize vs. summarize.** Scenes earn full dramatization when the conflict in them changes the story significantly. Events that move time without changing the story get summary.

"Over the next three weeks, nothing changed." That's summary. Six words. The scene you dramatize is the one where everything changes.

**Tension = shorter sentences.** As a scene approaches its crisis, sentences get shorter, paragraphs shrink, beats quicken.

---

## THE SCENE'S FORWARD MOMENTUM

The last beat of a scene must give the reader a reason to turn the page. Not a cliffhanger every time — a question, an unresolved tension, an implication the situation is going somewhere they need to follow.

Every scene ends with an unanswered question — even if neither the character nor the reader can name it. If the scene ends without leaving a question open, the reader has no momentum into the next scene.
