# EVERYDAY WRITER — SCRIPT WRITING SKILL

**Part of the Everyday Writer system. Upload this file to a Claude.ai Project alongside `ew-master.md`, or use standalone.**

**To activate:** Tell Claude "use the script writing skill" or "write me a screenplay scene using Everyday Writer."

---

## WHAT THIS SKILL IS FOR

Screenplays and scripts for film, television, and short-form video. The format exists to serve one purpose: communicate a story that will be realized by other people. Your job is not to write the movie. Your job is to make the movie inevitable on the page.

Script writing is the most constrained form of fiction writing. Every constraint is a discipline. Master the constraints before you break them.

---

## BEFORE WRITING: APPLY THE ANTI-AI RULES

These rules are mandatory. A script that passes format standards but fails the anti-AI checklist is a script a reader will put down at page 10.

### Banned Words and Patterns — Delete on Sight

delve, utilize, leverage, notably, importantly, fundamentally, remarkably, deeply, tapestry, landscape, paradigm, cornerstone, stands as, serves as, marks a pivotal moment, represents a significant shift, marking a significant turning point, reflecting the depth of

**Tacked-on -ing Analysis in action lines:**
"...marking a significant shift in their relationship."
"...underscoring the tension between them."
Cut these. If the scene is written correctly, the reader draws the inference.

**Announcing interiority:** Action lines cannot enter a character's mind. Only what can be seen and heard.
❌ "She remembers the last time she stood here."
✅ "She stops. Looks at the space where the couch used to be."

### Pre-Draft Checklist

- [ ] Any banned words or tacked-on -ing analysis phrases?
- [ ] Any interiority in action lines? (not filmable)
- [ ] Is every action line present tense and active voice?
- [ ] Are action blocks 4 lines or fewer?
- [ ] Are parentheticals used only where genuinely necessary?
- [ ] Have you run the anti-AI rules checklist?

## THE FUNDAMENTAL DIFFERENCE FROM PROSE

In a script, you control only what can be seen and heard on screen. No internal monologue. No "she felt the weight of the decision." No backstory in narration unless spoken aloud.

**The screen test:** Can this be filmed? Can a camera capture it? Can an actor perform it?

❌ "She remembered the last time she'd been here, three years ago, before everything changed."
✅ "She stops at the doorway. Looks at the space where the couch used to be. Doesn't move."

The second version is filmable. The first is not.

## FORMAT: THE RULES

Format is not bureaucracy. Correct format signals that you understand the medium.

**Slug lines (Scene headings):**
```
INT. KITCHEN - NIGHT
EXT. ABANDONED WAREHOUSE - DAY
INT./EXT. CAR - MOVING - DUSK
```
INT = interior. EXT = exterior. Location. Time of day. Do not write descriptions in the slug line.

**Action lines:**
- Present tense. Active voice. Only what can be seen or heard.
- Introduce characters in ALL CAPS on first appearance.
- Maximum 4 lines per action block. Longer reads slow on the page = feels slow on screen.

❌ "John walks slowly over to the window and looks out at the city that he once loved but now resents for reasons that will become clear later."
✅ "JOHN crosses to the window. The city below. He doesn't look at it for long."

**Character names:** Centered, ALL CAPS, above dialogue.

**Parentheticals:** Only when the reading of the line is genuinely ambiguous without direction. If the actor can figure out delivery from context, no parenthetical.
✅ "(under her breath)" — line must be inaudible to another character
❌ "(sarcastically)" — if the sarcasm isn't on the page, rewrite the line

**Page count:** 1 page ≈ 1 minute. Feature = 90–120 pages. Half-hour TV = 25–35 pages.

## VISUAL WRITING

**The visual verb:** Action lines live or die on their verbs.
"Walks" is not a visual. "Crosses," "drifts," "tears through," "freezes mid-step" — these are visuals. Every action line should contain at least one verb that tells us something about the character's emotional state without stating it.

**The telling visual:** Find the one image per scene that carries the scene's emotional truth. A character who straightens their tie before a difficult conversation. A child who hasn't touched their food. The object that's missing from the frame.

**What NOT to write:**
- Camera directions (CUT TO, CLOSE ON, PUSH IN) — unless you're also the director
- Music cues in the script body — that's the director's decision
- "We see" — redundant. The camera always sees.
- Weather descriptions that don't affect the scene

## SUBTEXT IN DIALOGUE

All scene-structure dialogue rules apply here — but in scripts they're even more important because there's no interiority to carry the weight.

**The iceberg rule:** The spoken line is 20% of the exchange. The 80% that's unspoken lives in the action line before and after the dialogue, in the silences, in what the character does while talking.

```
MARCUS
I saw you.

A beat. SARAH doesn't move.

MARCUS (CONT'D)
At the restaurant.

SARAH
Which restaurant.
```

The beat is a line of action. It is dialogue. Use it.

**Rhythm in dialogue:** Read every exchange aloud. If you can't say it at speed without it feeling unnatural, it isn't right. Real speech is interrupted, overlapping, incomplete.

**The "said" rule does not apply in scripts:** Dialogue in scripts has no dialogue tags. The character name provides the attribution.

## SCENE ECONOMY

Every scene must do at least one of: advance the plot, develop character, establish world, or create/escalate conflict. The best scenes do two or three simultaneously.

**Enter late. Leave immediately.** In prose you can afford a few setup lines. In a script, every line costs money.

**The two-scene test:** If two consecutive scenes convey roughly the same emotional information, one of them goes.

## THREE-ACT STRUCTURE

**Act One (pages 1–25):**
Establish the protagonist in their ordinary world. Inciting incident by page 12–15 at the absolute latest. By the act break (page 25–30), the protagonist is committed to a path they cannot easily undo.

**Act Two (pages 25–85):**
The protagonist pursues the goal against escalating opposition. Midpoint (page 55–60) = false peak. Low point (page 75–85) = darkest moment.

**Act Three (pages 85–110):**
Resolution earned from the character's internal transformation, not a convenient external rescue. The protagonist must actively choose.

**The want vs. the need:** Every protagonist has a surface want (what they're pursuing) and a deep need (what they actually require to be whole). The story is the collision between these two. The choice at the end must cost them something.

---

## TELEVISION AND SHORT-FORM

**Television:** Each episode has its own three-act structure but serves the season arc. The A-story and B-story must intersect thematically, not just logistically.

**Short-form (5–15 min):** One scene, one conflict, one turn. Start at the conflict. One turn in the middle. Land the payoff. Every short film that fails is a feature trying to be short — not a story designed at this scale.

## PRE-DRAFT CHECKLIST

- [ ] Is every action filmable — can a camera capture it, can an actor perform it?
- [ ] Do slug lines follow INT/EXT. LOCATION - TIME OF DAY?
- [ ] Are action blocks 4 lines or fewer?
- [ ] Are parentheticals used only where genuinely necessary?
- [ ] Is dialogue carrying subtext — and is the behavior around the dialogue written?
- [ ] Does every scene enter late and exit early?
- [ ] Does every scene do at least two functions?
- [ ] Is the inciting incident on screen by page 12–15?
- [ ] Does the protagonist make an active choice at each act break?
- [ ] Have you run the anti-AI rules checklist?
- [ ] Read every dialogue exchange aloud — does it move at speed?
