## WHAT THIS SKILL IS FOR

Website copy, landing pages, and homepage writing. Copy that converts visitors into readers, signups, or buyers — without lying, manipulating, or sounding like a SaaS onboarding wizard wrote it.

Web copy has one job: get the right person to take the right action. Everything else is decoration.

---

## BEFORE WRITING: APPLY THE ANTI-AI RULES

These rules are mandatory. Run the checklist before presenting any output.

### Banned Words — Delete on Sight

delve, utilize, leverage, robust, streamline, harness, certainly, notably, importantly, fundamentally, remarkably, deeply, foster, empower, showcase, underscore, highlight, tapestry, landscape, paradigm, synergy, ecosystem, framework, cornerstone, stands as, serves as, marks a pivotal moment, represents a significant shift, groundbreaking, revolutionary, transformative, seamlessly, cutting-edge, state-of-the-art, world-class

### Patterns to Kill in Web Copy

**The Corporate Stiffness Pattern:** "Our comprehensive suite of integrated solutions empowers teams to achieve synergistic outcomes at scale." No human speaks this way. No human trusts it.

**The Faux-Casual Pattern:** "Hey there! We know how much it sucks when your content just... doesn't land. We've been there too, friend." Forced intimacy is worse than formality.

**Vague Benefit Claims:** "Thousands of writers use this every day." → "Used by 4,200 independent newsletter writers, including [Name] ([Newsletter])."

**Unnamed Testimonials:** "John D., Entrepreneur" is not a testimonial. "Maya Rodriguez, who writes [Newsletter Name] for 12,000 subscribers" is.

**Generic CTAs:** "Learn more" / "Get started" — answer what action, what outcome, what cost.

### Pre-Launch Checklist

- [ ] Any banned words?
- [ ] Any corporate stiffness or faux-casual overreach?
- [ ] Does every benefit claim have specific proof?
- [ ] Are all testimonials named and specific?
- [ ] Is each CTA clear about action, outcome, and cost?
- [ ] Does the opening sentence do real work?
- [ ] Is there passive voice where an active subject exists?

## THE HIERARCHY OF A PAGE

Web readers do not read — they scan. They enter with a question: "Is this for me, and is it worth my time?" Your copy has roughly 5 seconds to answer yes before they leave.

**The hierarchy:**

**1. Headline:** The single most important sentence on the page. State the specific benefit or outcome in plain language.

❌ "Redefining the future of content creation."
✅ "Newsletter templates that don't sound like every other newsletter."

**2. Subheadline:** Supports the headline by adding one layer of specificity. What exactly does it do, for whom?

**3. Above-the-fold body:** 2–3 sentences maximum. Confirms the reader is in the right place. Addresses their most immediate concern.

**4. Social proof:** Specific, named, placed near the claim it supports — not dumped at the bottom.

**5. CTA:** One action per section. Clear, specific, low-friction language.

**Below the fold:** Everything that earns the skeptic. Features, how it works, more proof, objection handling, FAQ.

---

## HEADLINE RULES

Test every headline: could it appear on a competitor's site without being wrong? If yes, it's too generic.

**Specificity ladder:**
- Generic: "Better writing tools"
- Better: "Writing tools for independent creators"
- Good: "Ship your newsletter in half the time without sounding like AI wrote it"

**The benefit, not the feature:**
- Feature: "AI-powered grammar check"
- Benefit: "Write without stopping to fix every sentence"

Lead with the benefit. Explain the feature as the mechanism.

---

## TONE: DIRECT, NOT CASUAL

**Corporate stiffness:** "Our comprehensive suite of integrated solutions empowers teams to achieve synergistic outcomes at scale." Nobody speaks this way. Nobody trusts it.

**Faux-casual overreach:** "Hey there! We know how much it sucks when your content just... doesn't land. We've been there too, friend." Forced intimacy is worse than formality.

**Direct:** "This does X. It works because Y. Here's what happens when you use it." The reader is an adult. Write to them as one.

---

## SPECIFICITY AND PROOF

Every claim on a website is a bet. Vague claims pay out nothing. Specific claims build trust.

**The proof test:** After every benefit claim, ask: where's the proof? If you can't point to a number, a named example, a specific mechanism, or a testimonial from a real person — the claim is a gesture, not an argument.

❌ "Thousands of writers use this every day."
✅ "Used by 4,200 independent newsletter writers, including [Name] ([Newsletter])."

**Named testimonials only.**
"John D., Entrepreneur" is not a testimonial. "Maya Rodriguez, who writes [Newsletter Name] for 12,000 subscribers" is a testimonial. Unnamed quotes read as invented. Named quotes read as real.

---

## CTA ARCHITECTURE

One CTA per section. The action must be specific and the friction must be named honestly.

**Bad CTAs:**
- "Learn more" — learn more about what? How much more?
- "Get started" — started with what? What does that involve?
- "Join the community" — what community? What does joining require?

**Good CTAs:**
- "Start your free 14-day trial — no credit card required"
- "Download the template (PDF, 2 pages)"
- "Subscribe — one email per week, unsubscribe anytime"

The CTA must answer: what am I doing, what do I get, and what does it cost me?

---

## OBJECTION HANDLING

Every visitor who doesn't convert has an objection. Your copy's job is to anticipate and address the most common three before the reader has to ask.

**Common objections:**
- "Is this for someone like me?" — Address with specific audience language early
- "Is this worth the cost?" — Address with specific ROI or comparison
- "Can I trust this?" — Address with named proof and honest limitation statements

**Honest limitation statements build trust:**
"This works best for solo writers and small teams. If you're managing a content operation with 50+ people, you'll want something more enterprise-scale."

Acknowledging what you're not for makes the reader trust what you claim you are for.

---

## MICROCOPY

The small copy — button labels, form field hints, error messages — is where brand voice either shows up or disappears.

**Button labels:** Describe the action from the user's perspective.
❌ "Submit" → ✅ "Send my application"
❌ "Click here" → ✅ "See the full breakdown"

**Form field hints:** Tell the reader exactly what format you want and why.

**Error messages:** State what went wrong and what to do.
❌ "Invalid input" → ✅ "That email format doesn't look right — check for spaces or typos."
